## What is Biosafety Assessment?

Biosafety assessment is a systematic evaluation of biological risks associated with laboratory operations involving infectious agents, biological materials, and related procedures. The BioRAM biosafety assessment tool provides a structured, quantitative approach to identifying, evaluating, and mitigating biological hazards in research, diagnostic, and production facilities.

This assessment methodology helps laboratory safety officers, principal investigators, and facility managers make informed decisions about containment requirements, safety protocols, and risk mitigation strategies.

<Note>
The biosafety assessment is available in both the standalone Biosafety.raml model (Java application) and the integrated BioRAM-2022 Excel tool. Choose the format that best fits your workflow and technical requirements.
</Note>

## Assessment Structure and Sections

The biosafety assessment is organized into logical sections that build a comprehensive risk profile:

### Section 1: Facility Information
Establishes baseline information about the laboratory, institutional context, and regulatory environment.

### Section 2: Agent Characterization
Detailed evaluation of the biological agent(s) including classification, risk group assignment, and hazard properties.

### Section 3: Procedures and Techniques
Analysis of specific laboratory activities, equipment use, and potential exposure pathways.

### Section 4: Risk Mitigation Measures
Documentation of containment features, safety equipment, administrative controls, and PPE.

### Section 5: Training and Personnel
Assessment of staff qualifications, training programs, and health surveillance.

<Tip>
Complete the assessment in order, as later sections may reference information from earlier sections. Save your work frequently to preserve your responses.
</Tip>

## Risk Score Calculation

The biosafety assessment employs a quantitative scoring methodology that integrates multiple risk factors:

### Scoring Components

**Hazard Score**
- Based on agent characteristics, pathogenicity, and potential consequences
- Weighted by infectious dose and transmission efficiency
- Adjusted for genetic modifications or novel properties

**Exposure Probability**
- Influenced by procedure types and volumes handled
- Modified by containment effectiveness
- Adjusted for operational practices and controls

**Risk Mitigation Score**
- Credits for appropriate containment features
- Personnel training and competency factors
- Administrative and engineering controls
- Emergency preparedness measures

### Overall Risk Calculation

The tool combines these components using validated risk algorithms:

```
Risk Score = (Hazard × Exposure Probability) / Mitigation Effectiveness
```

Risk scores are normalized to a standard scale and categorized into risk levels:

## Interpreting Results

The assessment generates a comprehensive risk report that includes:

### Risk Summary Dashboard
- Overall risk level with supporting rationale
- Contributing factors ranked by impact
- Comparison to recommended biosafety level
- Identified gaps and deficiencies

### Detailed Risk Analysis
For each assessment section, the report provides:
- Individual component scores
- Key risk drivers and concerns
- Areas of strength and weakness
- Specific recommendations for improvement

<Warning>
A calculated risk score should not be the sole basis for safety decisions. Use the assessment as one input alongside regulatory requirements, institutional policies, and expert consultation.
</Warning>

## Biosafety Levels (BSL) Context

The biosafety assessment results help determine appropriate biosafety level designation:

### BSL-1: Basic Level
**Agents**: Well-characterized, no known disease potential in healthy adults

**Practices**: Standard microbiological techniques, minimal special containment

**Equipment**: Open bench work, PPE as needed for general protection

**Facility**: Standard laboratory suitable for teaching and research

---

### BSL-2: Enhanced Level
**Agents**: Moderate hazard to personnel and environment

**Practices**: BSL-1 plus limited access, biohazard warning signs, sharps precautions

**Equipment**: Biosafety cabinets for aerosol-generating procedures, PPE including lab coats and gloves

**Facility**: BSL-1 plus autoclave available, handwashing sinks

---

### BSL-3: High Containment
**Agents**: Indigenous or exotic agents with potential for aerosol transmission, serious or lethal disease

**Practices**: BSL-2 plus controlled access, decontamination of all waste, medical surveillance

**Equipment**: BSL-2 plus physical containment devices for all procedures, respiratory protection as needed

**Facility**: Separated from general traffic, self-closing double-door access, directional airflow, HEPA-filtered exhaust

---

### BSL-4: Maximum Containment
**Agents**: Dangerous and exotic agents with high individual risk of life-threatening disease, aerosol transmission, or unknown transmission

**Practices**: BSL-3 plus entrance through change room, shower on exit, all materials decontaminated on exit

**Equipment**: BSL-3 plus Class III biosafety cabinet or positive pressure suits with dedicated air supply

**Facility**: Separate building or isolated zone, dedicated supply and exhaust systems, multiple containment barriers

<Note>
The biosafety assessment evaluates whether proposed operations are appropriately matched to the designated biosafety level and identifies any upgrades or modifications needed for compliance.
</Note>

## Mitigation Recommendations

Based on assessment findings, the tool generates prioritized recommendations across multiple categories:

### Engineering Controls
- Facility modifications or upgrades
- Biosafety cabinet installation or certification
- HVAC and filtration system improvements
- Autoclave and decontamination equipment
- Architectural barriers and access control

### Administrative Controls
- Updated or enhanced SOPs
- Training program development
- Medical surveillance program elements
- Access control policies
- Emergency response procedures

### Personal Protective Equipment
- PPE selection based on exposure risk
- Fit testing and user seal checks
- Proper donning and doffing procedures
- PPE maintenance and replacement schedules

### Operational Modifications
- Alternative procedures or techniques
- Reduced volumes or concentrations
- Enhanced supervision requirements
- Buddy system implementation
- Scheduled reassessment intervals

<Tip>
**Prioritization Approach**

1. **Immediate**: Critical safety gaps requiring immediate action before work proceeds
2. **Short-term**: Important improvements to be implemented within 1-3 months
3. **Long-term**: Enhancements to further reduce risk over 6-12 months
4. **Continuous**: Ongoing monitoring, training, and program maintenance
</Tip>

## Best Practices for Assessment

### Preparation
- Gather relevant documentation (SOPs, training records, equipment certifications)
- Involve key personnel (PI, safety officer, facilities staff)
- Review current biosafety level designation
- Identify all biological agents to be assessed

### Completion
- Answer all questions thoroughly and honestly
- Provide supporting documentation where available
- Note areas of uncertainty for follow-up
- Involve subject matter experts as needed

### Follow-up
- Review results with institutional biosafety committee
- Develop action plan for identified deficiencies
- Document corrective actions and timelines
- Schedule reassessment after major changes
- Maintain assessment records per institutional policy

<Warning>
Reassessment is required when:
- New biological agents are introduced
- Procedures or techniques change significantly
- Facility modifications occur
- Adverse events or near-misses are reported
- At minimum every 3 years for ongoing activities
</Warning>
